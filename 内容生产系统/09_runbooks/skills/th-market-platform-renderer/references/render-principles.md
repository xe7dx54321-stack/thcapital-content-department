# Render Principles

This file defines the difference between a **master draft** and a **platform-ready handoff**.

## 1. A master draft is not the same as a publish-ready platform asset

A master draft proves:

- the argument exists
- the evidence exists
- the structure basically works

A platform-ready handoff proves:

- the first screen is designed
- the reading rhythm is designed
- emphasis is designed
- visual insertion points are designed
- the human publisher knows how to execute

## 2. Rendering is not cosmetic

Rendering changes:

- how fast the reader understands the piece
- where they pause
- what they remember
- what they quote or share

So rendering is a business layer, not a beauty layer.

## 3. Every render should answer five questions

1. What does the first screen need to do?
2. Which lines deserve visual emphasis?
3. Where should visuals enter?
4. Where should trust / risk / citations appear?
5. What does the human publisher still need to manually complete?

## 4. Typical render deltas

Examples of render deltas:

- shorten long paragraphs into `2-4` line chunks
- pull a key judgment into a quote block
- move a risk note nearer to the relevant claim
- convert a long Xiaohongshu text section into card bullets
- break a long X explanation into thread steps

## 5. Failure modes

A weak render usually looks like:

- the file is “clean” but still generic
- the platform version reads like copied long-form text
- there is no first-screen design
- the human publisher still has to guess where to emphasize or insert visuals
